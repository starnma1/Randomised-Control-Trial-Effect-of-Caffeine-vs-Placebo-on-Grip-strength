# Randomised-Control-Trial-Effect-of-Caffeine-vs-Placebo-on-Grip-strength

This randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial investigated the acute effect of caffeine ingestion on maximal voluntary grip strength in healthy adults. The study was conducted under standardized laboratory conditions to minimize environmental and procedural variability.

A total of 12 participants were recruited and randomly assigned to receive either caffeine or a placebo. The intervention consisted of a single acute dose of caffeine administered prior to testing. The placebo condition involved an identical capsule containing an inert substance. Both participants and investigators were blinded to allocation throughout data collection.

Grip strength was assessed using a calibrated hand dynamometer. Multiple trials were performed per participant to account for measurement variability and potential learning effects associated with repeated device use. Testing procedures, including posture, hand positioning, and rest intervals between trials, were standardized across all participants.

The primary outcome was maximal grip strength (measured in kilograms). The study aimed to determine whether acute caffeine supplementation leads to a statistically and practically significant increase in maximal voluntary grip force compared to placebo.

Data were analyzed using a linear mixed-effects modeling approach to account for repeated measurements within participants and inter-individual variability. Statistical significance was evaluated using a two-sided alpha level of 0.05.

This study contributes to the growing body of literature examining the ergogenic effects of caffeine on neuromuscular performance, with a specific focus on isometric strength output in small muscle groups.
